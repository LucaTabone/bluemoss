About

Greetings! I'm Alex, a dedicated technology advocate who's experienced in selling highly-technical SaaS-based B2B products and services. My expertise lies in Sales Engineering, Technical Account Management, and other more-technical, solutions-oriented roles. I excel in cross-functional collaboration with sales, product, and marketing teams, and I thrive on crafting tailored pre- and post-sale solutions and driving revenue through upsell, cross-sell, net-new acquisitions, and renewals.

My professional interests encompass a wide array of areas including AI/ML/DL, big data, automation, dev-ops/SDLC, Java, Python, macro vs micro service architecture, and always more. I also like to delve into research on topics like "product evangelism," product growth strategy, sales enablement, and sales strategy. I am skilled in understanding customer pain-points, overcoming technical hurdles during the sales cycle, handling objections, and successfully closing deals.

With a background in Computer Science and Music, I love working with folks who are building creative, practical and transformative technologies that help bring humanity forward. On my own time, I enjoy creating various electronic gadgets, painting, and building furniture. As a musician, I creatively leverage various software tools for both professional and artistic pursuits, and am an experienced storyteller. You can find my work under the creative alias PaLaTyPus and other undisclosed, secretive pseudonyms. I look forward to connecting with you!

    This article explores intersections between place, race/ethnicity, and gender amongst American Twitter users and makes an argument that studying the intensity of tweets provides insights into how and why particular groups tweet. Given recent events in American political life such as the shooting inFerguson, Missouri and the reactions by young, urban African Americans on Twitter, understanding the role of race, place, gender, and age is important. We observed the time between tweets of urban American Twitter users and explored whether the medium may be providing traditionally marginalized groups, such as young Black men, with potential avenues for mobilizing communication and access to resources.
